The MITM technique requires you to install a CA certificate as trusted in your OS or browser. This is a significant security action: your browser will trust any certificate signed by that CA, which means the security of this entire setup depends on keeping your private key secret. Understand the risks before you proceed.

<Warning>
  Never use a certificate file (`mycert.crt`) from someone else. Never share your private key (`mycert.key`) with anyone. Generate your own personal certificate and keep both files private.
</Warning>

## Why certificate security matters

When you install a CA certificate as a trusted root, you are telling your OS and browser to trust every TLS certificate that CA signs — for **any** domain. This is by design, and it is what allows Xray to decrypt and re-encrypt your Google traffic locally.

The risk is:

- **If someone gives you their certificate and you install it as trusted**, they can intercept all of your HTTPS traffic — not just Google. Every website you visit over HTTPS becomes readable to whoever controls that certificate's private key.
- **Your private key (`mycert.key`) is what allows Xray to decrypt traffic.** If someone else has your private key, they can also decrypt your traffic, even without physical access to your device.

This is why the project documentation states explicitly: do not give these two files to anyone, and do not take them from anyone — generate and use them yourself.

## Rules to follow

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="1. Always generate your own certificate">
    Use the included `certificate_generator.bat` script (Windows), the `xray tls cert -ca` command, or an online tool such as the self-signed certificate generator at [regery.com](https://regery.com/en/security/ssl-tools/self-signed-certificate-generator). Never accept a `mycert.crt` or `mycert.key` file from another person.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="2. Never share your private key">
    Do not share `mycert.key` with anyone under any circumstances. This file is what gives Xray — and anyone who obtains it — the ability to decrypt traffic passing through the MITM layer.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="3. Keep your certificate file private">
    Do not share `mycert.crt` unnecessarily. While less dangerous than the private key on its own, it is still your personal CA. Sharing it without the key does not immediately expose your traffic, but it is still poor practice.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="4. Rotate your certificate if you suspect compromise">
    If you believe your private key has been exposed, take the following steps:
    1. Generate a new certificate pair.
    2. Remove the old `mycert.crt` from your trusted root store (OS and any browsers where it was installed).
    3. Replace `mycert.crt` and `mycert.key` in your Xray `bin` folder (or v2rayNG asset files on Android).
    4. Restart Xray / v2rayN / v2rayNG.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Scope of MITM

It is important to understand what Xray actually intercepts with this configuration:

- Xray's MITM only decrypts traffic that matches the routing rules — specifically `geosite:google` and `domain:googlevideo.com`.
- All other traffic is sent directly without MITM decryption.
- The decryption and re-encryption happen **locally on your machine only**. No traffic leaves your device in a decrypted state.

The config's routing table sends everything else (Iranian domains, private IPs, and unmatched traffic) directly through without touching it.

## Verifying your certificate

After installation, you can confirm your certificate is correctly trusted in the right store.

- **Windows**: Open **Manage computer certificates** (run `certlm.msc`) and check that your certificate appears under **Trusted Root Certification Authorities** → **Certificates**, not under the Current User store.
- **Android**: Go to **Settings** → **Security and privacy** → **More security settings** → **View security certificates** → **User**. Your certificate should appear in this list.

<Tip>
  The self-signed certificate generated by `xray tls cert -ca` is not trusted by anything by default — it only becomes powerful when you install it as a trusted root. Keep your certificate files and this setup private.
</Tip>
